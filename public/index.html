2 min — 1 light warm-up set of your first exercise (50% of working weight, 12-15 reps).

🏃 Extended Cardio Finisher

20–25 min total: Start with 5 min walking, then alternate 30 sec fast walk / 30 sec slow jog for 10–15 min, then cool down with 5 min easy walk. This interval style burns more fat than steady-state while staying beginner-friendly.

Progressive Overload Rule: Once you can complete all sets at the top of the rep range with good form, increase weight by 2.5 kg (upper body) or 5 kg (lower body) the following week. Never sacrifice form for weight.
Rest Days — Days 6 & 7
Rest days are where your muscles actually grow and repair. Don't skip them. Here's exactly what to do on your two off days each week.

Active Recovery (Day 6 — Saturday)

  • 🚶
    30–40 min walk outdoors — Not a hike, not a jog. Just a relaxed walk. Sunlight and movement boost recovery hormones and reduce cortisol.
  • 🧘
    15–20 min stretching or yoga — Focus on hip flexors, hamstrings, chest, and shoulders. These get tight from sitting and lifting. Try a beginner yoga flow on YouTube.
  • 🎾
    Optional: light recreational sport — A casual game of badminton, swimming, cycling at an easy pace, or shooting hoops. Keep it fun, not competitive.
  • 🫧
    Foam rolling / self-massage — Spend 10 min foam rolling your quads, IT band, upper back, and glutes. This reduces soreness and improves mobility for the next week.

Full Rest (Day 7 — Sunday)

  • 😴
    Prioritize sleep — Aim for 7–9 hours. Sleep is the #1 muscle recovery tool. No training adaptation happens without quality sleep.
  • 🍳
    Meal prep for the week — Cook chicken, rice, roast vegetables, boil eggs. Having food ready prevents bad decisions Monday through Friday.
  • 📝
    Review your training log — Note which weights you used, where you felt strong, and where you struggled. Plan next week's progressions.
  • 💧
    Hydrate extra — Drink 3–4 liters of water throughout the day. Add a pinch of salt if needed for electrolytes. Hydration directly impacts next week's performance.
  • 🧠
    Mental reset — Do something you enjoy that isn't fitness-related. Read, watch a movie, spend time with friends. Mental freshness prevents burnout.
Key Rule: If you're still very sore from the week, take both days as full rest. Soreness that lasts 3+ days means you're either not sleeping enough, not eating enough protein, or going too heavy too soon. Dial it back and let your body adapt.

6 to 12 Month Progression Plan
Your body adapts every 4–8 weeks. This roadmap phases your training so you keep making gains without plateauing. Each phase builds on the last.
Phase 1 · Months 1–2
Foundation & Habit Building

Goal: Learn every exercise with perfect form. Build the habit of showing up 5 days a week. Don't chase heavy weights — chase proper movement patterns.

Training: Follow the weekly split exactly as written above. Use moderate weights where you could do 2–3 more reps after your set ends (known as "reps in reserve" or RIR 2–3). This prevents injury while your joints, tendons, and muscles adapt.

Cardio: Stick with 10–15 min post-workout walks/bike as prescribed. On weekends, add a 20–30 min walk outdoors.

Fat Loss Target: Aim to lose 0.5–1 kg per week through a mild calorie deficit of 300–500 kcal below maintenance. Use a TDEE calculator to find your baseline.

Phase 2 · Months 3–4
Progressive Overload Phase

Goal: Start pushing yourself. Add weight every 1–2 weeks on compound lifts (bench, squat, deadlift, rows). Aim for RIR 1–2 (only 1–2 reps left in the tank).

Training Changes: Add 1 extra set to compound movements (e.g., bench goes from 4×8 to 4×10, then add weight). Introduce Barbell Hip Thrusts on Day 4. Add chin-ups or assisted chin-ups on Day 3.

Cardio Upgrade: Increase post-workout cardio to 15–20 min. Add one 25-min LISS (Low Intensity Steady State) session on Saturday — brisk walk, easy swim, or cycling.

Expected Strength Gains: Bench press +10–15 kg, Squat +15–20 kg, Deadlift +15–25 kg from starting weights.

Phase 3 · Month 5–6
Intermediate Transition

Goal: You're no longer a pure beginner. Weekly weight jumps slow down — switch to adding weight every 2 weeks or adding reps before adding weight.

Training Changes: Introduce a deload week every 4th week (reduce all weights by 40%, keep reps the same). Add drop sets on isolation exercises (lateral raises, curls, tricep pushdowns). Begin incorporating supersets to save time and boost calorie burn.

Cardio Upgrade: Add one standalone 30-min cardio session mid-week — incline treadmill walk or stair climber. Post-workout cardio remains 15–20 min.

Body Composition: By month 6, expect to have lost 6–10 kg of fat (if diet is consistent) and gained 3–5 kg of muscle. Take progress photos and measurements — the scale alone won't tell the full story.

Phase 4 · Months 7–8
Strength Block

Goal: Shift focus toward lower rep, heavier weight on big lifts to break through plateaus. This builds true strength that carries over to all exercises.

Training Changes: On Days 1 and 2, move compounds to 5×5 heavy (bench, squat). On Day 4, deadlift goes to 5×3 heavy. Keep isolation work at 3×10-15 for muscle growth. Add paused reps on bench (1 sec pause at chest) and tempo squats (3 sec descent).

Cardio: Maintain current cardio volume. Don't increase — heavy strength training is already very metabolically demanding. Keep 2–3 cardio sessions (15–20 min each) per week total.

Strength Targets: Bench 1RM roughly bodyweight, Squat 1RM roughly 1.2× bodyweight, Deadlift 1RM roughly 1.5× bodyweight. These are achievable beginner milestones.

Phase 5 · Months 9–10
Hypertrophy & Definition

Goal: Switch back to higher volume to grow muscle you've strengthened. This is where you start looking noticeably different.

Training Changes: Move to a true Push/Pull/Legs/Upper/Lower 5-day split. Increase total weekly sets per muscle group to 15–20. Add cable flyes, preacher curls, lateral raise variations, and rear delt flyes. Rep ranges become 8–15 for most exercises.

Cardio Strategy: If fat loss has stalled, add 2 short HIIT sessions per week (15 min: 20s sprint / 40s rest on bike or rower). Keep 1–2 LISS sessions. Total cardio: ~60–80 min per week.

Nutrition Adjustment: If you've been in a deficit for 6+ months, take a 2-week "diet break" eating at maintenance calories. This resets metabolic hormones and prevents burnout.

Phase 6 · Months 11–12
Consolidation & New Goals

Goal: Assess everything you've achieved. Test your strength maxes. Take comprehensive progress photos. Set goals for year two.

Training: Alternate 3 weeks of hypertrophy with 1 week of strength testing (work up to heavy singles/doubles on big 3 lifts). This keeps both qualities sharp. Continue the PPL or Upper/Lower split based on personal preference.

Cardio: Find a sustainable long-term cardio routine — 3 sessions per week of your preferred method (walking, cycling, swimming, sports). This is about lifelong heart health now, not just fat loss.

12-Month Realistic Results: If consistent with training and nutrition — 8–15 kg fat lost, 5–8 kg lean muscle gained, major strength improvements across all lifts, visibly different physique, improved energy, sleep, and confidence.

Nutrition & Recovery Tips
Training is only 50% of the equation. What you eat, how you sleep, and how you recover determine whether you actually see results.

🥩 Protein

Eat 1.6–2.2 g per kg of bodyweight daily. For a 75 kg person, that's 120–165 g of protein. Spread across 4–5 meals. Sources: chicken, eggs, fish, paneer, Greek yogurt, whey protein, lentils, tofu.

🍚 Carbs

Don't fear carbs — they fuel your workouts. Eat 3–5 g per kg bodyweight. Prioritize complex carbs: rice, oats, sweet potato, whole wheat roti, fruits. Have most carbs around your workout.

🥑 Fats

Keep fats at 0.8–1.2 g per kg bodyweight. Healthy sources: nuts, avocado, olive oil, ghee, fatty fish. Fats support hormones — don't go below 50 g/day.

💧 Water

Drink 3–4 liters per day. More if you sweat a lot. Dehydration kills performance and recovery. Carry a bottle everywhere. Add electrolytes if training in heat.

Essential Tips for Beginners
  1. Track your workouts. Use a simple notebook or phone app. Write down every exercise, weight, sets, and reps. Without tracking, you can't progressively overload.
  2. Sleep 7–9 hours every night. Muscle growth hormone peaks during deep sleep. Poor sleep = poor recovery = poor gains. Make this non-negotiable.
  3. Don't skip meals. Missing meals means missing protein. If you can't eat a full meal, have a quick shake or handful of nuts + boiled eggs. Consistency beats perfection.
  4. Form over ego. Nobody at the gym cares how much you lift. They respect good form. Bad form = injury = weeks of lost progress. Watch every tutorial link and practice with lighter weights first.
  5. Be patient. Visible changes take 8–12 weeks. Strength gains come faster (you'll notice within 3–4 weeks). Trust the process. Consistency over 12 months will transform your body.
  6. Limit alcohol. Alcohol directly impairs muscle protein synthesis and sleep quality. It's one of the biggest hidden saboteurs of gym progress. Limit to occasional social drinking.
  7. Supplements are optional. The only ones worth considering for beginners: whey protein (for convenience), creatine monohydrate (5g daily — well-researched and safe), and a multivitamin if your diet is limited.
  8. Don't compare your Day 1 to someone's Year 5. Everyone started where you are. The people who look great at the gym were once nervous beginners too. Focus on being better than last week's version of yourself.
Remember: This plan is a general guide. If your friend has any pre-existing injuries, medical conditions, or specific limitations, it's always a good idea to consult a doctor or certified personal trainer before starting. Listen to your body — mild soreness is normal, sharp pain is not.
